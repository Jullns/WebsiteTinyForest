Toxicity

The fire salamander’s radiant coloration indicates its toxicity; it secretes a neurotoxin called "samandarin" through the skin that can cause muscle seizures, shortness of breath, increased blood pressure and death. However, if not ingested, this toxin usually does not pose a danger to humans.

Description

The fire salamander (Salamandra salamandra) is an amphibian found in deciduous forests throughout Europe.
His skin is black with yellow spots, although there are many variations of patterns. While the larvae are aquatic, the adults cannot swim and spend their lives on land.
Fire salamanders range from 15 to 30 cm in length and average a weight of 30 to 50 g. Males and females do not differ in size or appearance.

Popularly called the common salamander or fire salamander, it is a common and abundant species with a large distribution in Europe, present in forests and humid woods. In France it is called a spotted salamander (salamandre tachetée), because of the bright yellow spots that cover its body. These spots are a clear warning to potential predators not to approach and, much less, to prove their wet and soft skin. Its glands produce potentially lethal neurotoxic substances. The conspicuous coloration of the fire salamander is considered an example of aposematism, which can be defined as an association of a warning sign with a defense mechanism such as toxicity, impalatability or nocivity.
