Family: Coccinellidae
Order: Coleoptera
Suborder: Polyphaga

Name: Ladybugs

Scientific name: Coccinella septempunctata

Stains and coloration

Ladybugs have the appearance of small semispherical, round or oval domes with spots. Their legs and antennae are short. Its beautiful distinctive stains and colors are meant to repel predators. Ladybugs can secrete fluid from the joints of the paws, which gives them a bad taste. Its coloration is probably a reminder to any animal that has ever tried feeding on this species: "my taste is awful." A ladybug under threat can pretend to be dead and secrete the unwearable substance to protect itself.

Agriculture

Most ladybugs voraciously consume insects that feed on plants, such as aphids, and thus help protect crops. Ladybugs lay hundreds of eggs in the colonies of aphids and other phytophagous pests. Upon hatching, the ladybug larvae immediately begin to feed.

Description

The seven-spot ladybird, scientifically named Coccinella septempunctata, is the most common ladybug species in Europe. This iconic insect is instantly recognizable by its dome-shaped body and bright red wing cases, which feature exactly seven black spots. These vivid colors act as an evolutionary warning to predators, a defense known as aposematism, signaling that the beetle tastes bitter. When threatened, they can also reflex-bleed a toxic fluid from their joints. As voracious predators, both adults and larvae play a vital role in biological pest control.

They feed primarily on aphids, scale insects, and mites, making them highly beneficial for agriculture and gardens. Coccinella septempunctata can adapt to various habitats, including fields, parks, and suburban gardens, as long as there is an abundant food supply. During winter, they overwinter in large groups under rocks, logs, or dense vegetation to survive freezing temperatures, emerging in spring to mate and lay eggs.
