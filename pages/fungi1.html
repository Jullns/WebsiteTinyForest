Family: Amanitaceae
Order: Agaricales
Genus: Amanita

Name: Mario mushroom

Scientific name: Amanita muscaria

Toxicity

With reference to the toxic and hallucinogenic properties of A. muscaria, literature is sometimes somewhat conflicting. According to Guzman (Hongos, Mexico, Limuras Balderas,1981), although A. muscaria is reputed to be very poisonous, its toxicity is not serious. When ingested, it causes vomiting and diarrhea and the intoxicated person recovers in a few hours. Calange [Setas (Hongos) Guia Ilustrada, Madrid, Mundi Prensa, 1979] refers to the fact that the mushroom is toxic but not deadly, contrary to what was believed in the past. Its muscarine content is scarce, and micetoatropin is its most dangerous poison. This would be the fundamental reason why it is not advisable to apply atropine sulfate in people with A. mascaria poisoning. Instead of inactivating muscarine, the product exacerbates symptoms. According to this author, poisoning must be fought with saline purgatives and stomach lavage, the hallucinogenic substances present in this mushroom being ibothemic acid, mucimol, which is a derivative of ibothemic acid by dehydration, and muscazone, all psychoactive.

Habitat

This ectomycorrhizal species is frequently associated with stands of birch ( Betula sp. ) or conifers ( Pinophyta ), such as pine forests, in acidic soils. It also occurs in scrubland, cork oak forests ( Quercus suber ), holm oak forests ( Q. rotundifolia ), and eucalyptus plantations (Eucalyptus sp.). It can form "fairy rings." It fruits from late summer to autumn.

Distribution

This species originates from the Northern Hemisphere. In mainland Portugal, it occurs mainly in coniferous forests , being distributed throughout the country.

Description

Morphologically, this fungus is a good example of Agaricales. It has volva, stipe, ring, pin, residual scales of the velum and well-developed blades on the lower face of the pin. Its well-developed basidiocarp can reach more than 20 cm in height and up to 20 cm in diameter from a pin or hat. The coloration of the pin varies from scarlet red to orange red, and may present, when still young, a phase in which predominates the yellowness green. Pileus 8-24 cm in diameter, egg-shaped when young, and convex, flat or slightly concave when ripe. Pale yellow surface to reddish orange or even scarlet. Usually dotted with numerous white or pale yellow warts or excrescents that sometimes lie in concentric circles; pronounced striped or cristate margins; fleshy white or pale yellow just below the cuticle or vividly colored upper layer. Lamelles, about 20 per cm linear and 8-15 mm wide, free or slightly arising in white or pale yellow wrinkles or narrow crests.

The cough is 10-20 cm long and 1-2 cm thick or diameter at the upper end; the basal part of the cough is thicker to form a bulb, encircled by irregularly broken white or pale yellow rings. Rings in the upper third of the spit, white, soft, at first protruding but then becoming dry and inconspicuous. It sometimes returns well defined, but often becomes inconspicuous or not evident with age, appearing, however, only as rings on the bulbous lower part of the stylus. Solitary or group fruiting and often arranged in the form of rings under various coniferous trees, in Europe and the United States. In Brazil, this mushroom has been associated only with plants of the genus Pinus.
