Family: Ophiocordycipitaceae
Order: Hypocreales
Genus: Ophiocordyceps

Name: The last os us Fungus

Scientific name: Ophiocordyceps unilateralis

Habitat

Ophiocordyceps unilateralis, also known as "zombie fungus", was discovered by the British naturalist Alfred Russel Wallace in 1859, and is now found predominantly in tropical forests such as the Amazon. It infects carpenter ants (Camponotus cruentatus) that feed on fungi and live in warm, humid environments that facilitate their proliferation. This relationship between the O. unilateralis fungus and the ant is the result of a long evolutionary process, and indicates that the insect carries the conditions necessary for the fungus to survive.

Medicine

Other species of Cordyceps fungi have beneficial qualities, including for human health - such as Cordyceps cicadae, Cordyceps sinensis and Cordyceps militaris, widely used by traditional Chinese medicine. The three species are described as producing compounds with different pharmacological properties: anti-inflammatory, antitumor, immunomodulatory, nephroprotective and hepatoprotective.

Description

This fungus actually transforms the ant into a zombie because it secretes neurotoxins inside the animal, which then compromise the functioning of the insect’s muscles, altering its behavior in the anthill. After being infected, the ant becomes bewildered and begins to search for more illuminated places, and ends up coming out of the anthill. In this process, she spreads the spores of the fungus that already take over her body.

The fungus infection - lasting 2 to 5 days - makes the body of the ant all whitish, with a mold-like appearance. Filled with microscopic spores inside and outside, the members of the ant begin to be paralyzed by neurotoxins. Unable to move, the ant dies and its inert body waste remains useful as food for the fungus. This process of reproduction of Ophiocordyceps unilateralis has guaranteed its survival.
