Family: Fomitopsidaceae
Order: Polyporales
Genus: Laetiporus

Name: Chicken of the woods

Scientific name: Laetiporus cincinnatus

Uses

The mushroom can be prepared in most ways that one can prepare chicken meat. It can also be used as a substitute for chicken in a vegetarian diet. Additionally, it can be frozen for long periods of time and retain its edibility. In certain parts of Germany and North America, it is considered a delicacy.

Visual

The fruiting bodies have a pale orange or salmon coloration on top. At the bottom (hymenophore), the pores are white or cream.

Description

Individual "shelves" range from 5 to 25 centimetres (2 to 10 inches) across. These shelves are made up of many tiny tubular filaments (hyphae). The mushroom grows in large brackets; some have been found that weigh over 45 kilograms (100 pounds). Young fruiting bodies are characterized by a moist, rubbery, sulphur-yellow to orange body sometimes with bright orange tips. Older brackets become pale and brittle almost chalk-like, mildly pungent, and are often dotted with beetle or slug/woodlouse holes.

It is considered by many to be the tastiest and softest among the "Forest Chicken" mushrooms. When young and fresh, its edges are softer and juicy. Soft and watery when young, becoming chalky and eventually crumbling easily; white; not changing when sliced. Always cook this mushroom thoroughly before consuming, as raw can cause gastrointestinal issues in some people.
